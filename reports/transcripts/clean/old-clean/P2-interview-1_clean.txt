Interviewer: Our research focuses on technology use while hiking. We want to understand what devices people use, the apps on their smartphones, and how these impact their hiking experience. In 2017, nearly 98% of participants hiked with their smartphone and about 95% with headphones. Now in 2024, we want to see what has changed. Can you tell me about your hiking experience and how you got started?

Participant: When I was younger, my family hiked a bit, but not long hikes. In college, friends started hiking every weekend in summer, and I joined them. That’s actually how I met my wife, so it was a good motivation. Living in Provo, there are many great hikes nearby, so it feels like a shame not to take advantage.

Interviewer: What benefits have you gained from hiking?

Participant: It’s one of my main ways to get outside and exercise since I don’t do many sports. It helps me stay healthy. Also, being in nature is calming and good for mental health. It’s just fun and something I enjoy doing.

Interviewer: How has being in nature helped your mental well-being?

Participant: It’s calming and helps reset my mind. Everyday life can get overwhelming with work or school, so it’s nice to realize there’s a whole world outside of that.

Interviewer: How long do you usually hike?

Participant: Usually between four and eight hours. I try to go every weekend during summer, about 75% of the time. In winter, I hike much less, maybe once or twice.

Interviewer: How is your winter hiking experience?

Participant: We bundle up with good jackets and stick to established trails where the snow is packed down. We use crampons for traction on the ice.

Interviewer: How do you track your distance and progress?

Participant: I don’t track it myself, but my wife uses Strava to track distance and time.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for hikes?

Participant: Usually the day before, sometimes earlier, we check the drive time, road conditions, trail length, steepness, and weather forecast. That helps us decide how much water and food to bring. We keep supplies like sunscreen and bug spray in our hiking bags.

Interviewer: Besides mental well-being, why do you hike every weekend in summer?

Participant: I just think it’s fun and a way to spend time with friends.

Interviewer: How do you feel different in nature compared to the city?

Participant: Nature is beautiful. I enjoy looking at natural formations, even small things like moss patterns or rock strata. It’s usually quiet except for wind and birds. I like thinking about how things ended up the way they are, like the geology.

Interviewer: How many times do you hike in a year?

Participant: About 15 to 20 times, roughly four hikes per month during the three or four months of summer.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for wildlife or unfamiliar animals on hikes?

Participant: I’ve lived in Utah all my life, so I’m familiar with local wildlife. I keep in mind animals to watch out for, like rattlesnakes or mountain lions. Sometimes we take pictures of plants or animals and later identify them with family or my wife, who knows a lot about plants.

Interviewer: On your recent hike, you used your phone four times. How does smartphone use affect your hiking experience?

Participant: Mostly I used it to check the time because I was worried about getting back in time for errands. Generally, my phone is the only device I carry. I use it for pictures, selfies, checking the time, and occasionally responding to calls or texts. Sometimes I check the weather app if I’m concerned about conditions.

Interviewer: Do you prefer hiking alone or with others?

Participant: I almost always hike with someone else.

Interviewer: What time of day do you usually hike?

Participant: We start early, around six or seven in the morning, and finish by one or two in the afternoon. Early starts help avoid the heat in summer.

Interviewer: How would your experience differ if hiking alone?

Participant: When alone, I usually do shorter hikes and let someone know where I’m going. I might listen to podcasts or music. Hiking with others is enjoyable because we can talk and point out interesting things to each other, which enhances the experience.

Interviewer: If hiking alone, what devices and apps would you use?

Participant: I’d let someone know my plans beforehand. On the trail, I’d probably listen to podcasts or music with earphones. I wouldn’t actively track my location but would carry my phone.

Interviewer: Have you ever hiked in areas without network coverage? How was that?

Participant: That happens often. I don’t notice much because I mainly use my phone for pictures and checking time, which don’t need a network. I only pay attention if I’m expecting a call or text and look forward to getting signal again.

Interviewer: Are there specific trails you prefer and why?

Participant: Yes, Silver Glance Lake in Provo Canyon is a favorite because of its diverse environments—wildflowers, forests, lakes—and a good challenge level. Mount Timpanogos is a classic; reaching the summit feels like a real achievement. For shorter hikes, Stuart Falls is great, especially for less experienced hikers, with a rewarding waterfall at the end.

Interviewer: Generally, what are essential devices for hiking?

Participant: A smartphone is key for GPS and maps. I recommend at least one person in the group have a trail map app like AllTrails. It’s rare to get lost, but GPS helps if you go off trail.

Interviewer: What safety measures do you take or recommend in case of emergencies?

Participant: Many phones now have satellite call features for emergencies, which can be lifesaving. Also, letting someone know your location and plans is crucial.

Interviewer: Do you personally think about these safety measures?

Participant: When hiking alone, I always tell someone where I’m going. I usually have my phone with me, so I can call for help if needed.

Interviewer: How did using the recording device for this study affect your hiking experience?

Participant: We joked about having to admit when we used devices. It made me more aware of my phone use. I might have glanced at my phone more without it, but overall it didn’t change my behavior much.

Interviewer: Did thinking about recording your device use change your experience in nature?

Participant: It made me more conscious about when I was using my phone, which had a small effect on reducing usage.

Interviewer: Did you use the guide app during your hike? How did it affect your experience?

Participant: We didn’t really use the guide app besides starting and stopping it, so it didn’t affect the hike.

Participant: Sounds great. Thank you very much.
