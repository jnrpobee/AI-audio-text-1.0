Interviewer: All right, so the purpose of this research is to have an in-depth understanding of the technology people use while hiking, what they use those technologies for, and how that impacts their hiking experience. To start, what does hiking mean to you generally?

Participant: I'm not a big hiker, but I do short hikes, like one to two miles. I like to go to different places, sometimes where there’s a waterfall. For me, hiking means a relaxing day and some exercise. I want a hike that’s not too rough but more than just plain walking. Exercise is a big part of hiking for me, along with enjoying the views.

Interviewer: When you go out to hike, what do you usually carry or prepare in terms of food, water, or electronics?

Participant: I always have my phone. I usually search for the hike’s length and difficulty level beforehand. I also carry my Apple Watch, my phone with GPS, and my AirPods. These three are what I usually bring.

Interviewer: You mentioned difficulty level. What kind of difficulty do you prefer for your hikes?

Participant: I prefer hikes that take one to two hours each way, so about four hours round trip max. I usually look for entry to medium difficulty hikes, nothing too tough.

Interviewer: Which areas have you hiked so far?

Participant: I’ve done the “Y” trail, Rock Canyon twice, and Stuart Falls here. In Pakistan, where I’m from, I’ve done several trails in Islamabad—there are about six major trails there.

Interviewer: Which of these would you want to hike again?

Participant: I’d go back to Stuart Falls because it ends at a waterfall. I like that. The “Y” trail’s view doesn’t change much, and I don’t like hikes that are just steep climbs for a short time. Stuart Falls has a good mix of climbing and walking, with changing scenery.

Interviewer: Does the changing scenery affect your hiking experience?

Participant: Yes, definitely. If the scenery stays the same for two hours, I get bored. I like it when the environment changes every 20 minutes or so, like going from forest to plains and ending at a waterfall.

Interviewer: Does the changing scenery affect how long you hike? Do you mind hiking longer if the scenery keeps changing?

Participant: No, I don’t mind hiking longer if the scenery keeps changing.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: Not very frequently these days, but about twice a month is a good estimate.

Interviewer: Why twice a month?

Participant: I run and swim almost every day, so hiking is more of a weekend activity for me. With student life, I might find a free weekend once a month or every two months to hike all day.

Interviewer: What makes you enjoy hiking?

Participant: I like going to different places and taking photos. Also, I usually hike with friends. I’ve never hiked alone.

Interviewer: Why don’t you hike alone?

Participant: I don’t know, I’ve never really thought about it. I do other exercises alone, like running and swimming, but hiking feels more like a group activity for me.

Interviewer: Does hiking with a group affect your experience of the environment? For example, does chatting with friends distract you from observing the scenery?

Participant: I still like it. We often discuss the scenery with the group, so it adds to the experience rather than taking away from it.

Interviewer: Do you face any challenges when hiking?

Participant: If it’s too steep or the altitude is too high causing oxygen issues, that can be challenging. But if I know the path and difficulty from apps or maps, I don’t usually face challenges. That’s why I always check the difficulty level before going.

Interviewer: How does technology help you with these challenges?

Participant: It gives me a sense of security because I have my phone in case of emergencies. I also research the hike beforehand—parking, routes, reviews, and what to carry. I use exercise apps on my watch to track calories, distance, and time. Sometimes I use headphones if it’s a long hike, otherwise, I chat with friends.

Interviewer: How safe do you feel when hiking?

Participant: On a scale of one to ten, I’d say five. I want to know the hike length and make sure I’m back before dark. I have emergency contact info set up on my phone, but I’m not overly concerned about safety.

Interviewer: Do you share your location with someone while hiking?

Participant: No, I usually hike with friends, so I don’t share my location.

Interviewer: What apps do you use when hiking?

Participant: Mostly Google Maps or Apple Maps for navigation, Spotify for music, YouTube if I want to show a video to someone, and social media apps like WhatsApp, Facebook, and Instagram for messages.

Interviewer: How do you use YouTube while hiking?

Participant: I use the video version to show something to friends if we’re discussing it. I don’t use it for music since I have Spotify.

Interviewer: Why do you prefer Spotify over other music apps?

Participant: I have a family plan that’s cost-effective, and I’ve been using it for about seven or eight years. I’m just used to it.

Interviewer: How often do you use social media while hiking?

Participant: I use it whenever I get notifications. I reply to messages or comments but don’t just scroll through reels or posts. If there’s no internet or notifications, I don’t open social media.

Interviewer: Apart from your phone, watch, and earphones, do you use any other devices when hiking?

Participant: No, just those three. I don’t carry anything else like a laptop.

Interviewer: Why do you use the health app on your watch?

Participant: I’m weight conscious and like to track calories burned versus eaten. It’s been a subconscious habit for about 10 years. I exercise daily to maintain my weight.

Interviewer: During your recent hike, how many times do you think you used your devices?

Participant: About 20 times during a one-hour hike. I took photos, checked apps on my phone and watch.

Interviewer: You used your devices more going up than coming down. Why?

Participant: Going up, I was checking paths on Google Maps because I wanted to try a smaller trail. I also took photos going up because I don’t usually take the same photos coming down. Coming down, I knew the path, so I used my phone less. It was also getting dark near the end, so I used a flashlight.

Interviewer: Why did you choose to hike at that time of day when it was getting dark?

Participant: I don’t like hiking in the scorching sunlight because I get sweaty. I plan hikes to be back around sunset. This one took a bit longer, so it got dark near the end, especially in the canyon where it’s usually dark.

Interviewer: Did hiking in the dark affect your experience?

Participant: I don’t like hiking in the dark and haven’t done it purposefully before. It was just because I misjudged the time.

Interviewer: You checked notifications during the hike. Do you usually respond to messages while hiking?

Participant: Yes, if it’s a message or comment on social media, I respond. I don’t interact much with Snapchat notifications though.

Interviewer: Does responding to messages or emails during a hike distract you from your surroundings?

Participant: Yes, I get lost in my phone for a minute but then come back to the hike.

Interviewer: How does that make you feel?

Participant: I wish I didn’t do it, but I feel kind of addicted to replying when I get notifications.

Interviewer: You played music at some point during the hike. Did you start listening to music from the beginning?

Participant: No, I started playing music on the way down when it was just me and my wife. We had been talking going up, so I played music on the way down because I knew the path and wasn’t thinking about much.

Interviewer: Did you use earphones for the music?

Participant: No, I played it out loud because it was just the two of us and no one else around. If there were other people, I would use earphones.

Interviewer: How often do you use earphones when hiking?

Participant: I always carry them, and if it’s a long hike where we stop talking, I use them. Sometimes I listen to voice messages on apps with earphones.

Interviewer: What kind of music do you prefer when hiking?

Participant: No particular genre. I usually play light songs from my Spotify playlist, which could be rap, poetry, or sad songs. I just play whatever is at the top of my playlist.

Interviewer: How did using the recording device during this study affect your hiking?

Participant: It made me more conscious of my device usage. I checked the time and notifications less because I could see them on the watch and didn’t need to pull out my phone as often.

Interviewer: Why did you stop using devices toward the end of the hike?

Participant: It was getting dark, and I didn’t want to risk tripping while looking at my phone.

Interviewer: Did the device usage make you more aware of how often you use your phone?

Participant: Yes, definitely.

Interviewer: Why did you choose the canyon for this hike?

Participant: It’s within walking distance from my house, and I prefer it over the “Y” trail because I like the views better.

Interviewer: Why did you take photos at those specific points?

Participant: I took photos where the scenery changed, like at the trailhead and when the terrain looked different. I also took photos at sunset on the way back.

Interviewer: How does your device usage, like checking notifications and time, impact your engagement with nature?

Participant: I don’t feel detached from nature when using my phone. For me, everything, including device use, is part of the personal hiking experience.

Interviewer: Do you post your hiking photos on social media?

Participant: Yes, but never while hiking. I post them the next day or after I’m back home.

Interviewer: How does looking at those photos later help you connect with the place?

Participant: It helps me maintain memories and remember the hike and who I was with.

Interviewer: Thank you so much for your time and participation in this study.

Participant: Thank you.
