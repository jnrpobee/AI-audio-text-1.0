Interviewer: Thank you so much. Before we begin talking about your hiking experience to the Y, how would you define hiking?

Participant: I think hiking is anytime you go outside, especially in nature, on a trail that’s not paved. It’s about exploring, adventuring, taking in God’s creations, connecting and disconnecting. It’s different from walking on a sidewalk because you’re on a marked trail in nature. It can be very short too, even a quarter mile or so counts as hiking.

Interviewer: How long do you usually hike for?

Participant: On a single day, we usually hike 12 to 14 miles, but I’ve done as much as 36 miles in one day. For example, we did 26 miles hiking Kings Peak in one day. The shortest hikes I’ve done are about half a mile or less.

Interviewer: How do you usually prepare for your hikes?

Participant: It depends on the length and difficulty. For short hikes, maybe just water and a quick snack. For longer or more challenging hikes, like Kings Peak, I prepare more thoroughly—packing meals, extra clothes, and scouting the trail beforehand to understand the terrain and any tricky parts. Preparation also depends on who I’m hiking with and the purpose of the hike.

Interviewer: What experiences do you usually gain from hiking, and what motivates you to hike?

Participant: I love connecting with God, nature, and people. I enjoy seeing beautiful places like meadows, lakes, or waterfalls. Hiking helps me disconnect from life’s hustle and bustle and connect with myself and others. Motivation comes from the joy of the experience itself—seeing something amazing, gaining perspective, and the physical and mental benefits. There’s also a social aspect; hiking is a cheap, easy way to spend quality time with others and have meaningful conversations.

Interviewer: How does hiking impact your well-being?

Participant: Hiking positively impacts my happiness. Being outside in natural light and nature helps declutter my mind and improves my spatial awareness. It’s a time for communion with God, connecting with people, or just healthy solitude. Hiking offers a balance of being alone yet not lonely, or connected yet intimate. It’s very beneficial for overall well-being.

Interviewer: What time of day do you prefer to hike?

Participant: Usually in the morning, especially since we have eight kids and can go before they wake up. Morning hikes are cooler and more pleasant. I’ve also hiked at midnight, afternoon, and evening. Each time offers different experiences—like seeing a sunrise or stars at night, or different temperatures and wildlife. The time of day affects the trail conditions, people you meet, and your purpose for hiking.

Interviewer: How do the people and wildlife you encounter impact your hiking experience?

Participant: Mostly positive, but sometimes scary. For example, we once encountered a rattlesnake on the trail, which was nerve-wracking. We’ve seen mountain grouse that startle you, moose swimming in lakes, and mountain goats asserting their territory. Wildlife encounters can be amazing or intimidating. People on the trail are usually friendly and helpful, though some prefer solitude. You meet all kinds of personalities, from casual hikers to mountain experts.

Interviewer: Do you have safety concerns when hiking, and what measures do you take?

Participant: In bear country or if there have been recent sightings, I’m more cautious and might avoid certain areas. I try to be wise about wildlife, respecting their space and avoiding risky encounters. Most animals don’t want to bother you if you don’t bother them. For rattlesnakes and similar animals, I stay alert and give them space.

Interviewer: How does technology like your phone, earbuds, or watch impact your hiking experience?

Participant: It depends on the purpose. Devices can be a huge blessing for navigation, especially on unfamiliar or tricky trails. They help avoid getting lost and keep communication open with family. Listening to podcasts with my wife can enhance our conversations and make the hike more meaningful. Technology also helps track pace, elevation, and health metrics, which motivates me during workouts. Sometimes, technology can be annoying if it distracts from disconnecting, so I use it selectively to enhance rather than detract from the experience.

Interviewer: Does listening to podcasts or other audio content disengage you from nature?

Participant: Sometimes it can, but usually not. We listen mostly on familiar trails, so it doesn’t distract from exploring. If it ever feels like a distraction, we turn it off to reconnect with nature. The content we listen to is intellectually stimulating, which feels like having a conversation rather than just entertainment, so I still feel present in nature.

Interviewer: Besides podcasts, what else do you listen to while hiking?

Participant: I sometimes listen to General Conference talks or audiobooks that are intellectually stimulating, like parenting podcasts or things that help me think differently. I avoid fiction or fantasy because hiking itself is an escape. Occasionally, I listen to music, but I find that talks help me keep a steady pace better than music.

Interviewer: What apps do you usually use on your phone during hikes?

Participant: We use All Trails a lot for trail info and conditions, and the weather app for forecasts. Audible and podcast apps are common for listening. I also use the Gospel Library app. After hikes, I sync my watch data with my phone to review elevation, time, and other stats. Sometimes I use a notes app to jot down thoughts if I’m reflecting or pondering during the hike.

Interviewer: Why do you take pictures on hikes, and what do you do with them?

Participant: Pictures help capture moments I want to remember—the beauty of a sunrise, a cool animal, or a special place. They serve as tangible memories and help share the experience with others. My wife and I often take pictures together to remember our shared experiences. Photos also help reconnect with the place later and share stories with friends or family.

Interviewer: How do pictures help you engage with a place on subsequent visits?

Participant: They provide benchmarks to see how a place changes over time—different seasons, lighting, or even the people you’re with. Photos remind me of past experiences and memories, making the place feel familiar and special. Sometimes they inspire me to revisit a spot because of a memorable moment captured there.

Participant: Yes, we stopped at a rock near the summit of Y Mountain that overlooks Rock Canyon and Kai Peak. We’ve taken pictures there before, so it’s a way to memorialize the accomplishment and see how we look now compared to before.
