Interviewer: Our research focuses on technology use while hiking. We want to understand what devices people use, the apps on their smartphones, and why they use them. In 2017, nearly 98% of participants hiked with their smartphones and about 95% with headphones. Now in 2024, we want to see what has changed regarding devices and apps and how they impact the hiking experience and time in nature. Can you tell me about your hiking experience? What got you involved in hiking?

Participant: When I was younger, my family went hiking quite a bit, though not very long hikes. My parents and grandpa did a lot. In college, a group of friends started hiking every weekend during summer, and I tagged along. That’s actually how I met my wife, so that was a big motivation. Also, living in Provo, there are many great hikes nearby, so it seems a shame not to take advantage of them.

Interviewer: What benefits have you gained from hiking over the years?

Participant: Hiking is one of the main ways I get outside and exercise since I don’t do many sports. It helps me stay healthy. It’s also good for mental health—being in nature feels calming and helps reset my mind. Sometimes we get caught up in work or school, and hiking reminds me there’s a whole world outside of that.

Interviewer: How long do you usually hike?

Participant: Typically between four and eight hours. I try to go every weekend during summer, about 75% of the time. In winter, I hike much less, maybe once or twice.

Interviewer: How is hiking in winter?

Participant: We bundle up with good jackets and usually stick to established trails where snow is packed down. We use crampons—those wraparound shoes with spikes—for traction.

Interviewer: How do you track your distance and location during hikes?

Participant: I don’t track it myself, but my wife usually starts a Strava activity to track distance and time.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for hikes?

Participant: Usually the day before, sometimes earlier, we check how far it is to drive, road conditions, and if we need a vehicle that can handle dirt roads. We look at the hike’s steepness, length, and weather forecast to decide how much water and food to bring. We keep supplies like sunscreen and bug spray in our hiking bags to grab easily.

Interviewer: Besides mental well-being, why do you hike every weekend in summer?

Participant: I just think it’s fun and a way to spend time with friends.

Interviewer: How would you describe your time in nature and how it feels different from city life?

Participant: Nature is very beautiful, which has a big effect. I enjoy looking at natural formations, even small things like moss patterns or rock strata. It’s usually quiet except for sounds of wind and birds. I like thinking about how things ended up the way they are, like the twisted geology.

Interviewer: How many times do you usually hike in a year?

Participant: During the three or four months of summer, about four hikes per month, so roughly 15 to 20 hikes a year.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for wildlife or unfamiliar animals on hikes?

Participant: I’ve lived in Utah all my life and am familiar with local wildlife. I keep in mind animals to watch out for, like rattlesnakes or mountain lions. Sometimes I take pictures of plants or animals we see, and later my wife or family help identify them. My wife especially likes knowing about plants and shares that knowledge with me.

Interviewer: On your recent hike, you spent about two hours on the trail and used your phone four times. How does smartphone use affect your hiking experience?

Participant: Mostly I used my phone to check the time because I was worried about getting back in time for errands. Generally, my phone is the only device I carry. I use it for pictures, checking the time, sometimes responding to calls or texts, and occasionally checking the weather app.

Interviewer: Do you usually hike alone or with others?

Participant: Almost always with someone else.

Interviewer: What time of day do you usually hike?

Participant: We start early, around six or seven in the morning, and finish by one or two in the afternoon. Early morning hikes are cooler, especially in summer.

Interviewer: How would your experience differ if you hiked alone? How does hiking with others affect your experience?

Participant: When alone, I might listen to podcasts or music. I’d probably do shorter hikes and let someone know where I’m going. Hiking with others is enjoyable because we can talk and point out interesting things to each other, which enhances the experience.

Interviewer: If hiking alone, what devices and apps would you use?

Participant: I’d let someone know my plans beforehand. On the trail, I’d likely listen to podcasts or music with earphones and carry my smartphone.

Interviewer: Have you ever hiked in areas without network coverage? How was that experience?

Participant: That happens often. I don’t usually notice because I mostly use my phone for pictures or checking time, which don’t require network. I only pay attention if I’m expecting a call or text and look for signal at certain points.

Interviewer: Are there specific locations you prefer to hike and why?

Participant: Yes, I like Silver Glance Lake in Provo Canyon because of its diverse environments—wildflower fields, forests, lakes—and a good challenge level. Mount Timpanogos is a classic; reaching the top feels like a big achievement. For shorter hikes, Stewart Falls is great—it's a relatively easy hike with a rewarding waterfall, good for less experienced hikers.

Interviewer: Can you share your experience hiking Mount Timpanogos and your preferred hiking difficulty?

Participant: The first time I tried Timpanogos, we didn’t reach the saddle due to snow and being underprepared. Another time with my mom, we got closer but turned back because the steep drop and tired legs made it risky. After my mission, I summited it a few times, including a sunrise hike where we hiked through the night but didn’t quite reach the peak. I prefer hikes around eight miles that take a good portion of the day. Above ten miles starts to feel hard for me.

Interviewer: How safe do you feel when hiking?

Participant: I consider myself pretty safe. I’m not an adrenaline junkie and avoid risky behavior. Trails can have steep drop-offs, but I use hiking poles for stability and carry enough water. I usually have water left over at the end.

Interviewer: What devices do you consider essential for hiking?

Participant: A smartphone is key for GPS and maps. I recommend at least one person in the group have a trail map app like AllTrails. GPS helps if you get off trail. Most hiking needs can be met with a smartphone.

Interviewer: What safety measures do you suggest for emergencies on hikes?

Participant: Many phones now have SOS features that connect to satellites for emergency calls. I know friends who used this feature to get help. Also, letting someone know your location before hiking is important.

Interviewer: Do you personally think about safety measures before hiking?

Participant: When hiking alone, I always let someone know where I am. Other than that, I don’t usually think about it much but always have my phone to call for help if needed.

Interviewer: How did using the recording device to track your device usage affect your hiking experience?

Participant: We joked about having to admit to the phone when using devices, which made me more aware of my usage. I might have looked at my phone more without it because glancing quickly is easy, but waiting for the device to record usage made me pause. Overall, it didn’t change my behavior much.

Interviewer: Did recording your device usage change your experience of being in nature?

Participant: It made me more conscious about when I was using my devices, which was a subtle but present thought during the hike.

Interviewer: Did you use the guide app for navigation during this hike?

Participant: Besides starting and stopping it, we didn’t really use the guide app, so it didn’t affect our navigation.
