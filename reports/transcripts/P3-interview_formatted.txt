Interviewer: Before we get into your hiking experience last week, what does hiking generally mean to you?

Participant: I enjoy hiking as a way to do things that are good for me. I like being outdoors, away from the computer and work, to relax and think without constant noise. I enjoy seeing different plants, animals, and views. I also like to paint, so I often take pictures on hikes to paint later.

Interviewer: When you go on a hike, how do you prepare?

Participant: I bring enough food, water, and first aid supplies. I wear layers, carry hiking poles, boots, and a backpack. I make sure my phone is fully charged and usually bring an extra battery pack for my phone or flashlight.

Interviewer: What benefits do you gain from hiking?

Participant: It helps me get physically fit, stronger, and improves my cardio health. I enjoy hiking with others because it’s a good time to talk and bond. Mentally, I feel refreshed, calmer, and ready to do other things.

Interviewer: When hiking with friends and chatting, does that distract you from the goal of feeling refreshed?

Participant: Sometimes I focus more on the conversation and miss some sights, but the topics usually aren’t stressful, so it still helps me relax. We also have periods of silence during hikes.

Interviewer: What differences do you notice between hiking alone and with friends?

Participant: When alone, I think more and notice my surroundings more. Sometimes I hike without my phone or listen to podcasts. With friends, there’s more social interaction.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: Almost every weekend when the weather is nice, not in winter.

Interviewer: How long are your hikes?

Participant: Usually a couple of hours. This year I’ve been busier, so about two to three hours. In past years, up to six or more hours.

Interviewer: How many hikes do you do in a year?

Participant: Probably 20 to 30, hiking from spring to fall.

Interviewer: Do you hike the same places or different locations?

Participant: About half and half. Some places I revisit yearly, but I also look for new spots.

Interviewer: What motivates you to return to the same places?

Participant: I enjoy those places a lot. Many are close by, which is convenient. I like seeing how the hike changes through seasons and tracking my own progress.

Interviewer: Do seasonal changes affect your well-being when hiking?

Participant: Yes, I appreciate different things in each season. No one season is better, but each offers unique experiences.

Interviewer: When hiking alone, how do you handle safety?

Participant: I take precautions. I only hike trails I know well and avoid new or unfamiliar ones alone. I stick to shorter, well-traveled hikes where I see other people. I use an app called Strava to share my GPS location with family and tell them my expected return time.

Interviewer: Besides Strava, do you use other apps while hiking?

Participant: Mainly my phone camera and sometimes my Fitbit watch to check steps or heart rate.

Interviewer: Do you use any other devices on hikes?

Participant: No, just my phone and Fitbit.

Interviewer: What do you use the camera for?

Participant: To capture nice views, interesting patterns, animals, or plants. I use the photos later for painting or identifying plants.

Interviewer: How do these photos help you connect back to the places?

Participant: I upload them to Google Photos, which sends reminders of when and where I was. Looking back at pictures helps me remember the experience better than written notes.

Interviewer: Why don’t you use other hiking devices?

Participant: My phone and Fitbit cover my needs. I’m not sure what else is available, but I’d try new devices if they seemed helpful.

Interviewer: Could you hike safely without these apps?

Participant: Sort of. Without location sharing, I’d stick to smaller, busier places and shorter hikes. I wouldn’t feel confident going far alone.

Interviewer: What are your preferences for hike difficulty?

Participant: I like challenging hikes. Time is the biggest factor. The longest I’ve done is about 16.5 miles. A typical summer hike is 6-7 miles. I enjoy shorter hikes too, but prefer longer ones when I have a full day.

Interviewer: How do you define a longer hike?

Participant: Eight miles or more.

Participant: Sure.

Participant: I didn’t need to check the map often because I’ve done this trail before. Also, it was warm, so I moved quickly and didn’t stop much for pictures.

Interviewer: When you checked the time, did you use your Fitbit or phone?

Participant: Just the time, I used my Fitbit. I might glance at the phone time when checking GPS, but usually Fitbit for time only.

Interviewer: You took only two pictures on this hike. Why so few?

Participant: Mostly we were in the trees, so fewer views. I took one at a lookout and one of a plant I wanted to identify.

Interviewer: How does being in a natural environment far from human living feel?

Participant: I really enjoy it. It’s alive with plants and animals, different sounds and smells. It’s very different from my daily experience, so I notice more.

Interviewer: Do you prefer hiking on established trails or making your own path?

Participant: I stick to established trails to preserve the environment and for safety. Sometimes trails are overgrown and require some bushwhacking, but mostly I stay on the trail.

Interviewer: Did you use your extra battery on this hike?

Participant: No, I didn’t need it on the hike but used it while driving back.

Interviewer: Why carry an extra battery?

Participant: My phone battery drains quickly, especially on long hikes. Also, as a precaution in case of emergencies, to keep my phone or flashlight charged.

Interviewer: Is an extra battery necessary?

Participant: Not for short hikes, but very useful for long ones.

Interviewer: How was your experience using the hiking device for recording?

Participant: It didn’t make a huge difference. I looked at my watch less because I didn’t want to interrupt recording. It made me more conscious of device use. It also made me think about keeping a hike journal or notes.

Interviewer: Did using the device distract you from your nature experience?

Participant: Somewhat, yes.

Interviewer: How comfortable were you using the device while hiking with others?

Participant: I was hiking with my husband who used the same device, so it felt normal between us. Sometimes it felt strange when others saw me talking to myself.

Interviewer: Did checking the time affect how long you wanted to stay hiking?

Participant: Yes, especially on this hike because my husband had an errand. We had to monitor time to get back on schedule.

Interviewer: Does checking the time distract from the wellness you seek in nature?

Participant: Yes, unfortunately.

Interviewer: Could you hike without checking the time and still meet your goals?

Participant: It depends. Not knowing the time when you have an appointment would be more stressful. I get a better experience when I don’t have to worry about time. Sometimes I check time to gauge the heat of the day rather than to plan return.

Participant: The trail is less used and overgrown in parts, so I check to make sure I’m on the right path. Also, my phone sometimes loses GPS signal, so I check to ensure it’s recording.
