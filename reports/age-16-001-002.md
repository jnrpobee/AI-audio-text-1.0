## Summary
**Short:** The participant is an experienced hiker who set a summer goal of completing 50 new hikes (each at least three miles), which led them to hike twice a day to catch up. They primarily use a smartphone (AllTrails/maps/camera) for navigation on unfamiliar routes and a Garmin watch to track mileage, pace, and time, but they feel technology can distract from their desired connection with nature and faith. Safety practices include telling a group chat which trail they’re on, sometimes carrying bear spray on longer hikes, and potentially carrying a gun when alone on very long hikes. During the study hike, heavy map-checking and many photos were driven by an unmarked section near the top and a changing sunset, while the recording device made them feel self-conscious and less immersed in nature.

**Long:** The participant describes hiking as a frequent, goal-driven activity and a key outlet from school stress—combining nature, exercise, reflection on “God’s creations,” and social connection—while also accommodating knee problems that prevent running. This summer they pursued an ambitious target of 50 different, new-to-them hikes (minimum three miles), completing 42 so far and choosing less popular routes after exhausting well-known ones; they prepare with a dedicated hiking bag, check elevation, and usually invite others, though they hiked alone for the study because recording around friends felt awkward. Their core tech stack is a smartphone and Garmin watch: the phone (especially AllTrails) is essential on unfamiliar trails to avoid getting lost and to gauge elevation/turnaround points, while the watch supports mileage, speed, and time management; however, frequent checking—especially when tired or on poorly marked terrain—can create a “time crunch” and reduce their sense of freedom and engagement with nature. They avoid music to stay aware of animals and because, as a music major/producer, listening becomes analytical work rather than relaxation; notifications are occasionally checked but considered distracting, and social media posting is rare and often delayed due to poor service. Safety is salient, particularly as a woman hiking alone: they do not share live location but do tell others the specific trail, may carry bear spray for hikes over five miles, and might carry a firearm on very long solo hikes; encounters like a deer or a skeleton are exciting but increase caution. The study’s recording device notably altered behavior by prompting stops and verbal explanations, increasing self-consciousness (e.g., about breathing) and pulling attention away from the hike compared with their normal quick glances at the watch and more natural focus on surroundings.
