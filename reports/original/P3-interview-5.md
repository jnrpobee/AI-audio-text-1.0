# Interview Analysis

## Transcript
Interviewer: Before we get into your hiking experience last week, what does hiking generally mean to you?

Participant: I enjoy hiking as a way to do things that are good for me. I like being outdoors, away from the computer and work, to relax and think without constant noise. I enjoy seeing different plants, animals, and views. I also like to paint, so I often take pictures on hikes to paint later.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for your hikes?

Participant: I bring enough food, water, and first aid supplies. I wear layers, use hiking poles, boots, and carry a backpack. I make sure my phone is fully charged and usually bring an extra battery pack for my phone or flashlight just in case.

Interviewer: What benefits do you gain from hiking?

Participant: It helps me get physically fitter, stronger, and improves my cardio health. I enjoy hiking with others because it’s a good time to talk and bond. Mentally, I always feel refreshed, calmer, and ready to tackle other tasks afterward.

Interviewer: When hiking with friends and chatting, does that distract you from feeling refreshed?

Participant: Sometimes I focus more on the conversation and miss some sights, but often the topics aren’t related to my stress, so it still helps me relax. We usually have periods of chatting and periods of silence to take it all in.

Interviewer: How does hiking alone compare to hiking with friends?

Participant: When alone, I tend to think more and notice my surroundings better. Sometimes I go without my phone, other times I listen to podcasts or talks.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: Almost every weekend when the weather is nice, usually spring to fall, not in winter.

Interviewer: How long do your hikes usually last?

Participant: Typically two to three hours this year, though in past years I’ve done longer hikes up to six hours or more.

Interviewer: How many hikes do you do annually?

Participant: Probably 20 to 30 hikes a year, mostly on weekends from spring to fall.

Interviewer: Do you hike the same places or different locations?

Participant: About half are familiar places I visit yearly, and half are new locations I try to explore.

Interviewer: What motivates you to return to the same hiking spots?

Participant: I enjoy those places a lot, and proximity is a factor since they’re close by. I also like seeing how the hike changes with the seasons and tracking my own progress over time.

Interviewer: Do seasonal changes affect your well-being when hiking?

Participant: Yes, I appreciate different things in each season. No one season is better, but each offers unique experiences.

Interviewer: When hiking alone, how do you manage safety concerns?

Participant: I take precautions by only hiking trails I know from previous group hikes. I avoid new or unfamiliar trails alone and stick to shorter, well-traveled paths where I’m likely to see others. I use an app called Strava to share my GPS location with family so they know where I am and when to expect me back.

Interviewer: Besides Strava, do you use any other apps to connect with nature or stay focused?

Participant: I use my phone camera a lot to capture scenes or plants I want to paint or identify later. I also use a Fitbit to track steps and heart rate.

Interviewer: Do you use any other devices on hikes?

Participant: No, just my phone and Fitbit.

Interviewer: What is your main purpose for using the camera on hikes?

Participant: To capture beautiful views, interesting patterns, animals, or plants I want to identify or paint later.

Interviewer: How do your photos help you connect back to the places you’ve hiked?

Participant: I upload them to Google Photos, which sends reminders of when and where I was. Looking back at pictures helps me remember the experience more vividly than just written notes.

Interviewer: Why don’t you use other hiking devices?

Participant: My phone and Fitbit cover my needs, and I’m not sure what else is available. If I learn about something useful, I might try it.

Interviewer: Without apps like Strava and the camera, do you think you could hike safely alone?

Participant: Somewhat. I’d probably stick to shorter, well-populated trails and be out for less time. I wouldn’t feel as confident going farther without location tracking.

Interviewer: What are your preferences regarding hike difficulty?

Participant: I like challenging hikes. Time availability is the biggest factor. If I have a full day, I enjoy longer hikes—up to about 16.5 miles. In summer, a typical hike is around six or seven miles. Shorter hikes are enjoyable too, but longer ones are preferred when possible.

Interviewer: Do you prefer hiking on established trails or making your own path?

Participant: I stick to established trails to help preserve the environment and for safety. Sometimes trails are overgrown and require some bushwhacking, but mostly I stay on the path.

Interviewer: You mentioned carrying an extra battery. Did you use it on your last hike?

Participant: No, I didn’t need it on the hike but used it while driving back.

Interviewer: Why carry an extra battery?

Participant: My phone battery drains quickly since it’s old. On longer hikes, I might need to recharge. It’s also a precaution in case something happens and I need to keep my phone or flashlight powered.

Interviewer: Is an extra battery necessary?

Participant: Not for short hikes, but very useful for longer ones.

Interviewer: How was your experience using the device to record your hike? Did it distract you?

Participant: It didn’t make a huge difference. I actually checked my watch less often because I had to take out the device to record. It made me more conscious of how I used it. It also made me think about keeping a hike journal or notes, which could be interesting.

Interviewer: Did using the device take away from your experience in nature?

Participant: Somewhat, yes.

Interviewer: How comfortable were you using the device while hiking with others?

Participant: Since my husband was using the same device, it felt natural. We reminded each other when to record. It felt a bit strange when other people saw me talking to myself, though.

Interviewer: Did checking the time affect how long you wanted to stay hiking?

Participant: Yes. On the last hike, we had to get back for an errand, so we checked the time to make sure we left in time.

Interviewer: Does checking the time distract from the wellness you seek in nature?

Participant: Unfortunately, yes.

Interviewer: Could you hike without checking the time and still enjoy it?

Participant: It depends. Not knowing the time when you have an appointment would be more stressful. But if I don’t have somewhere to be, I get a better experience without worrying about time. Sometimes I check the time just to be aware of the heat of the day.

Interviewer: You used the map a few times on a trail you know well. Why?

Participant: The trail is less used and sometimes overgrown, so I check to make sure I’m on the right path. Also, my phone sometimes loses GPS connection, so I check to ensure it’s recording properly.

Interviewer: Thank you for participating. We will send you a gift card as compensation once it’s ready.

Participant: Sounds good. I enjoyed participating.

Interviewer: We appreciate your time. We’ll reach out when the gift card is ready.

Participant: Thank you. Have a good evening.

Interviewer: You too. Goodbye.

Participant: Bye.
