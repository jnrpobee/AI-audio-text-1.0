## Summary
**Short:** The participant is an experienced hiker who, over the past month, has been hiking twice daily to catch up on a personal goal of completing 50 new hikes (each at least three miles). They rely primarily on a smartphone (AllTrails for navigation) and a Garmin watch (mileage/pace tracking), using technology heavily on unfamiliar or poorly marked trails but preferring to hike with minimal tech when they know the route. Technology is framed as necessary for safety and logistics (not getting lost, estimating time left), yet also as a distraction that reduces their connection to nature, spirituality, and social interaction. Safety considerations—especially hiking alone as a woman—include telling others the planned route, carrying bear spray on longer hikes, and sometimes carrying a firearm depending on conditions.

**Long:** The interview centers on how an avid hiker balances motivation, safety, and technology use during hikes. The participant reports hiking frequently—twice a day recently—to meet a structured goal of completing 50 different, new hikes of at least three miles, which also pushes them toward less popular trails they haven’t done before. Preparation includes packing a dedicated hiking bag, checking elevation, and typically inviting friends (though they avoided bringing others during the study due to awkwardness of recording). Their core devices are a smartphone and Garmin watch: AllTrails is used to prevent getting lost and to gauge elevation/turnaround decisions, while the watch supports mileage and pace tracking; they do not listen to music to stay aware of surroundings and because, as a music major, music prompts analytical attention rather than relaxation. Data patterns (frequent map checks and many photos) are explained by an unmarked trail section near the top and a changing sunset that warranted multiple angles; familiarity with a trail reduces both map-checking and photo-taking. The participant describes a tension between hiking as an escape—nature, exercise, social connection, and spiritual reflection—and the time pressure created by checking miles/pace to fit hikes into a busy schedule. Safety is managed more through pre-hike communication than live location sharing (messaging a group chat with the intended hike), and as a woman hiking alone they may carry bear spray for longer routes and occasionally a gun, emphasizing “better safe than sorry.” Finally, the study’s recording device is described as uniquely intrusive: it increased self-consciousness, required stopping to narrate actions, and pulled attention away from nature compared to their normal, more fluid hiking routine.
