# Interview Analysis

## Transcript
Interviewer: Our research focuses on technology use while hiking. We want to understand what devices people use, the apps on their smartphones, and why. In 2017, nearly 98% of participants hiked with their smartphone and 95% with headphones. Now in 2024, we want to see what has changed. Can you tell me about your hiking experience and what got you involved?

Participant: When I was younger, my family went hiking often, though not long hikes. My parents and grandpa did a lot. In college, friends started hiking every weekend in summer, and I joined them. That’s how I met my wife, which motivated me to keep hiking. Living in Provo, we have many great hikes nearby, so it feels like a shame not to take advantage.

Interviewer: What benefits have you gained from hiking over the years?

Participant: It’s a major way I get exercise since I don’t do many sports. It helps me stay healthy. Also, being in nature is good for mental health. It’s calming and helps me reset from daily stresses like work or school. It’s just something I enjoy.

Interviewer: How long do you usually hike?

Participant: Typically between four and eight hours. I try to go every weekend during summer, about 75% of the time. In winter, I hike much less, maybe once or twice.

Interviewer: How is hiking in winter?

Participant: We bundle up with good jackets. Winter hikes are usually on established trails where snow is packed down. We use crampons—spiked shoe covers—for traction.

Interviewer: How do you track your distance and progress?

Participant: I don’t track it myself; my wife usually starts a Strava activity to track distance and time.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for hikes?

Participant: Usually the day before, sometimes earlier, we check drive time, road conditions, and if a car that handles dirt roads is needed. We look at the hike’s length, steepness, and weather forecast to decide how much water and food to bring. We keep supplies like sunscreen and bug spray in our hiking bags.

Interviewer: Besides mental well-being, why do you hike regularly?

Participant: I just think it’s fun and a way to spend time with friends.

Interviewer: How do you feel different in nature compared to the city?

Participant: Nature is beautiful, and I enjoy looking at natural formations, even small things like moss patterns or rock strata. It’s usually quiet except for wind and birds. I like thinking about how things formed, like twisted rock layers in canyons.

Interviewer: How many times do you hike per year?

Participant: About four hikes per month during summer, so roughly 15 to 20 hikes annually.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for wildlife or unfamiliar animals on hikes?

Participant: I’ve lived in Utah all my life and know the local wildlife. I’m aware of threats like rattlesnakes or mountain lions. Sometimes I take pictures of plants or animals to learn more later. My wife knows a lot about plants and often points them out.

Interviewer: On your recent hike, you used your phone four times. How does smartphone use affect your hiking experience?

Participant: Mostly I used it to check the time because I was worried about errands later. Generally, my phone is the only device I carry. I use it for photos, checking time, occasionally responding to calls or texts, and sometimes checking the weather.

Interviewer: Do you usually hike alone or with others?

Participant: Almost always with someone else.

Interviewer: What time of day do you usually hike?

Participant: We start early, around six or seven in the morning, and finish by one or two in the afternoon. Early hikes are cooler, especially in summer.

Interviewer: How would your experience differ if hiking alone?

Participant: When alone, I might listen to podcasts or music. I’d do shorter hikes and let someone know my plans for safety. Hiking with others is enjoyable because we can talk and point out interesting things to each other.

Interviewer: What devices and apps would you use hiking alone?

Participant: I’d let someone know where I’m going beforehand. On the trail, I’d likely use a podcast app and earphones along with my smartphone.

Interviewer: Have you ever hiked in areas without network coverage? How was that?

Participant: It happens often. I don’t usually notice because I mainly use my phone for photos and checking time, which don’t need a network. I only notice if I’m expecting a call or text.

Interviewer: Are there specific trails you prefer?

Participant: Yes, Silver Glance Lake in Pearl Canyon is a favorite because of its diverse environments—wildflowers, forests, lakes—and a good challenge level. Mount Bonobos is a classic; reaching the top feels like an achievement. Stuart Falls is a shorter hike with a big waterfall, great for less experienced hikers.

Interviewer: Can you share your experience hiking Mount Bonobos?

Participant: The first time, with a group, we didn’t reach the peak due to snow and being underprepared. Later, with my mom, we got close but turned back because the steep drop and tired legs made it risky. After my mission, I reached the top with friends and have done it a few times since, including a sunrise hike where we got close to the peak.

Interviewer: What is your preferred hike difficulty?

Participant: Around eight miles, like Wymalen or Silver Glance Lake, which take a good portion of the day. Hikes over 10 miles start to feel hard for me.

Interviewer: How safe do you consider yourself while hiking?

Participant: Pretty safe. I’m not an adrenaline junkie and avoid risky activities. Trails can have steep drop-offs, but I use hiking poles for stability. I carry enough water and usually have some left at the end.

Interviewer: What devices do you consider essential for hiking?

Participant: A smartphone is key for GPS and maps. I recommend at least one person in the group have a trail map app like AllTrails. GPS helps if you get off trail.

Interviewer: What safety measures do you take in emergencies?

Participant: Many phones have satellite call features for emergencies, which some friends have used. The most important thing is letting someone know your location before hiking.

Interviewer: Do you personally think about safety measures before hikes?

Participant: When hiking alone, I always inform someone where I am. Otherwise, I don’t think about it much but always have my phone to call for help if needed.

Interviewer: How did using a device that records your phone usage affect your hiking behavior?

Participant: It made me more aware of when I used my phone. I might have glanced at it more without the recording device since recording adds a delay. We tried not to change our behavior too much.

Interviewer: Did recording your device usage change your experience of being in nature?

Participant: Yes, it made me think about whether I really needed to use my phone at certain times.

Interviewer: Did you use the guide app during your hike?

Participant: No, we didn’t use the guide app beyond starting and stopping it, so it didn’t affect our navigation.

Interviewer: Thank you for sharing your hiking experience and device use. We’ll prepare your gift card and email it to you.

Participant: Sounds great. Thank you very much.
