# Interview Analysis

## Transcript
Interviewer: Before we get into your hiking experience last week, what does hiking generally mean to you?

Participant: I enjoy hiking as a way to do things that are good for me. I like being outdoors, away from the computer and work, to relax and think without constant noise. I enjoy seeing different plants, animals, and views. I also like to paint, so I often take pictures on hikes to use as references for painting.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for your hikes?

Participant: I bring enough food, water, and first aid supplies. I wear layers, carry hiking poles, boots, and a backpack. I make sure my phone is fully charged and usually bring an extra battery pack for my phone or flashlight, just in case.

Interviewer: What benefits do you gain from hiking?

Participant: It helps me get physically fitter, stronger, and improves my cardio health. I enjoy hiking with others because it’s a good time to talk and bond. Mentally, I always feel refreshed, calmer, and ready to tackle other tasks after hiking.

Interviewer: When hiking with friends and chatting, does that distract you from feeling refreshed?

Participant: Sometimes I focus more on the conversation and might miss some sights, but usually the topics aren’t related to my stresses, so it still helps me relax. We often have periods of silence during hikes, so it balances out.

Interviewer: What’s the difference between hiking alone and hiking with friends?

Participant: When alone, I tend to think more and notice my surroundings better. Sometimes I hike without my phone, other times I listen to podcasts or talks.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: Almost every weekend when the weather is nice, from spring to fall, not in winter.

Interviewer: How long do your hikes usually last?

Participant: This year, about two to three hours due to being busier. In past years, I’ve done hikes up to six hours or more.

Interviewer: How many hikes do you do annually?

Participant: Roughly 20 to 30 hikes a year, mostly on weekends from spring to fall.

Interviewer: Do you hike the same places or different locations?

Participant: About half are familiar places I return to yearly, and half are new locations I seek out.

Interviewer: What motivates you to return to the same hiking spots?

Participant: I enjoy those places a lot, and many are within close driving distance. I also like seeing how the trail changes with the seasons and tracking my own progress over time.

Interviewer: Do seasonal changes affect your well-being when hiking?

Participant: Yes, I appreciate different things in each season. No one season is better, but each offers unique experiences.

Interviewer: How do you handle safety when hiking alone?

Participant: I take precautions by only hiking trails I know from group hikes. I avoid new or unfamiliar trails alone and stick to shorter, well-traveled paths where I see other hikers. I use an app called Strava to share my GPS location with family and tell them when I expect to return.

Interviewer: Besides Strava, do you use other apps while hiking?

Participant: I use my phone camera to take pictures and a Fitbit watch to track steps and heart rate.

Interviewer: Do you use any other devices on hikes?

Participant: No, just my phone and Fitbit.

Interviewer: What’s your main purpose for using the camera on hikes?

Participant: To capture pretty views, interesting patterns, animals, or plants I want to paint or identify later.

Interviewer: How do your photos help you connect back to your hikes?

Participant: I upload them to Google Photos, which sends reminders of where and when I was. Looking back at pictures helps me remember the experience more vividly than just written notes.

Interviewer: Why don’t you use other hiking devices?

Participant: My phone and Fitbit cover my needs, and I’m not sure what else is available. If I learn about something useful, I might try it.

Interviewer: Could you hike safely alone without these apps?

Participant: Sort of. Without location sharing, I’d stick to smaller, more populated trails and shorter hikes. I wouldn’t feel as confident going far alone.

Interviewer: What are your preferences for hike difficulty?

Participant: I like challenging hikes. Time availability is the biggest factor. The longest I’ve done is about 16.5 miles. In summer, a typical hike is 6-7 miles. I enjoy shorter hikes too but prefer longer ones when I have a full day.

Interviewer: Would you define a longer hike as eight or more miles?

Participant: Yes, that sounds right.

Interviewer: Regarding your recent hike, why did your Strava GPS start after some minutes instead of at the trailhead?

Participant: I thought I started it at the trailhead but might have made a mistake or delayed activating it.

Interviewer: You didn’t use your device much during the hike. Why?

Participant: I’ve done this trail before, so I didn’t need to check the map often. Also, it was warm that day, so I moved quickly and didn’t stop much for pictures.

Interviewer: When you check the time, which device do you use?

Participant: Usually my Fitbit for just the time. I might see the time on my phone when I pick it up, but Fitbit is my go-to for time checks.

Interviewer: You took only two pictures on this hike. Why so few?

Participant: The trail was mostly in the trees, so fewer photo opportunities. I took one at a lookout point and another of a plant I wanted to identify.

Interviewer: How do you feel being in an environment far from human living areas?

Participant: I really enjoy it. It’s alive with plants and animals, different sounds, sights, and smells. It’s very different from my daily environment, so I notice more about my surroundings.

Interviewer: Do you prefer hiking on established trails or making your own path?

Participant: I stick to established trails to help preserve the environment and for safety. Sometimes trails are overgrown and require some bushwhacking, but mostly I stay on the trail.

Interviewer: Did you use your extra battery on this hike?

Participant: No, I used it while driving back. My phone battery didn’t drain enough to need it.

Interviewer: Why carry an extra battery?

Participant: My phone is old and drains quickly, especially on long hikes. It’s also a precaution in case something happens and I need to keep my phone or flashlight charged.

Interviewer: Is an extra battery necessary?

Participant: Not for short hikes, but very useful for longer ones.

Interviewer: How was your experience using the device to record the hike? Did it distract you?

Participant: It didn’t make a huge difference. I actually looked at my watch less because I didn’t want to interrupt recording. It made me more conscious of device use. It also made me think about keeping a hike journal or notes.

Interviewer: Did being conscious of device use detract from your nature experience?

Participant: Somewhat, yes.

Interviewer: How comfortable were you using the device while hiking with others?

Participant: Since my husband was using the same device, it felt normal. We reminded each other to record things. Sometimes it felt a bit strange talking to myself when others were around.

Interviewer: Did checking the time affect how long you wanted to stay hiking?

Participant: Yes, especially on this hike because my husband had an errand. We had to monitor time to get back on schedule.

Interviewer: Did checking the time distract you from the wellness you seek in nature?

Participant: Unfortunately, yes.

Interviewer: Do you think it’s possible to hike without checking the time and still meet commitments?

Participant: It depends. Not knowing the time when you have an appointment might be more stressful. I get a better experience when I don’t have to worry about time. Sometimes I check time to gauge the heat of the day rather than to plan a return.

Interviewer: Why did you use the map on a trail you know well?

Participant: The trail is less used and overgrown in parts, so I wanted to make sure I took the right turns. Also, my phone sometimes loses GPS connection, so I check to ensure it’s still recording.

Interviewer: Thank you for participating. We will send you a gift card as compensation once it’s ready.

Participant: Sounds good. I enjoyed participating.

Interviewer: We appreciate your time. We’ll contact you when the gift card is ready.

Participant: Thank you. Have a good evening.

Interviewer: You too. Goodbye.

## Summary
**Short:** The participant views hiking as a beneficial activity for physical fitness, mental relaxation, and creative inspiration, often capturing photos to aid painting. They hike mostly on weekends from spring to fall, favoring both familiar and new trails, and take safety precautions when hiking alone, including using apps like Strava for location sharing. Device use during hikes is minimal but helpful for tracking and safety, though it can sometimes detract from the immersive nature experience. Hiking with others provides social bonding, while solo hikes allow deeper reflection and observation.

**Long:** The participant approaches hiking as a holistic activity that supports their physical health, mental well-being, and artistic interests. They hike regularly—about 20 to 30 times annually—primarily on weekends during favorable weather, balancing visits to familiar trails with exploration of new areas. Preparation includes carrying essential supplies and technology such as a fully charged phone, an extra battery, and a Fitbit for tracking steps and heart rate. Safety is paramount, especially when hiking alone, with the participant relying on known trails, GPS location sharing via Strava, and avoiding unfamiliar routes. While device use is limited to essential functions like photography and timekeeping, the participant acknowledges that attention to devices can sometimes interrupt the immersive experience they seek in nature. Social hikes offer opportunities for conversation and bonding without undermining relaxation, whereas solo hikes foster greater mindfulness and connection with the environment. The participant values seasonal changes in the landscape and prefers established trails to protect the environment. Time management during hikes is influenced by external commitments, occasionally causing distraction, but overall hiking remains a vital practice for rejuvenation and creative inspiration.

### Probing
