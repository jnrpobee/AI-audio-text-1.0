# Interview Analysis

## Transcript
Interviewer: Before we get into your hiking experience last week, what does hiking generally mean to you?

Participant: Hiking is a way I enjoy doing different things that are good for me. I like being outdoors, away from the computer and work, to relax and think without constant noise. I enjoy seeing different plants, animals, and views. I also like to paint, so I often take pictures on hikes to paint later.

Interviewer: When you go on a hike, how do you prepare?

Participant: I bring enough food, water, and first aid supplies—practical things. I make sure to have layers of clothes, hiking poles, boots, and a backpack. I also fully charge my phone and bring an extra battery pack for my phone or flashlight just in case.

Interviewer: What benefits do you gain from hiking?

Participant: Definitely physical fitness—getting stronger and better cardio health. I enjoy hiking with others because it’s a good time to talk and bond. Mentally, I always feel refreshed, calmer, and ready to tackle other things after hiking.

Interviewer: When hiking with friends and chatting, doesn’t that distract you from feeling refreshed?

Participant: Sometimes I focus more on the conversation and miss some sights, but often the topics aren’t related to my stress, so it still helps me relax. Usually, we alternate between chatting and silent moments to take it all in.

Interviewer: How does hiking alone compare to hiking with friends?

Participant: When alone, I tend to think more and notice my surroundings more. Sometimes I go without my phone; other times I listen to podcasts or talks.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: Almost every weekend when the weather is nice, not in winter.

Interviewer: How long are your hikes usually?

Participant: A couple of hours recently, though in past years I’ve done longer hikes up to six hours or more.

Interviewer: How many hikes do you do in a year?

Participant: Probably 20 to 30, hiking weekly from spring to fall.

Interviewer: Do you hike the same places or different locations?

Participant: About half and half—some favorite spots I return to yearly and some new places I try.

Interviewer: What motivates you to return to the same places?

Participant: I enjoy those places a lot, and many are within close driving distance. I like seeing how the hike changes through the seasons and tracking my own progress as I get stronger.

Interviewer: Do seasonal changes affect your well-being when hiking?

Participant: Yes, I appreciate different things in each season. No one season is better, but each offers unique experiences.

Interviewer: When hiking alone, how do you handle safety?

Participant: I take precautions. I only hike trails I’ve done before with a group, avoid unfamiliar or remote places, and stick to shorter, well-traveled hikes where I’ll see other people. I use an app called Strava to share my GPS location with family and tell them when I expect to return.

Interviewer: Besides Strava, do you use other apps or devices to connect with nature or stay focused?

Participant: I use my phone camera a lot to take pictures. I also have a Fitbit watch to track steps and heart rate.

Interviewer: Do you use any other devices when hiking?

Participant: No, just my phone and Fitbit.

Interviewer: What do you mainly use the camera for?

Participant: To capture pretty views, interesting patterns, animals, or plants I want to identify later. I paint from these photos and look up plant names when I get home.

Interviewer: How do the pictures help you connect back to the places you’ve hiked?

Participant: I upload them to Google Photos, which sends reminders of when and where I was. Looking back at pictures helps me relive the experience more vividly than just writing notes.

Interviewer: Why don’t you use other hiking devices?

Participant: My phone and Fitbit cover my needs. I’m not sure what else is out there, but I’d try something new if it seemed helpful.

Interviewer: Without apps like Strava and your camera, would you feel safe hiking alone?

Participant: Sort of. Without location sharing, I’d stick to shorter hikes in busier areas and be out less time. I wouldn’t feel as confident going farther away.

Interviewer: What are your preferences regarding hike difficulty?

Participant: I like challenging hikes. Time is the biggest factor. If I have a full day, I enjoy longer hikes—my longest was about 16.5 miles. Normally, summer hikes are around six or seven miles. Shorter hikes are fine too, but given a day off, I prefer longer ones. I consider eight miles or more a long hike.

Interviewer: Regarding your recent hike, I want to share your trail on screen. Is that okay?

Participant: Yes, that’s fine.

Interviewer: (Shares screen showing the Lionhead trail and attempts to pull up 3D view.)

Interviewer: I noticed your Strava GPS started a few minutes after your hike began. Why didn’t you start it at the trailhead?

Participant: I’m not sure. I thought I started it at the trailhead, but maybe I made a mistake or it took time to activate.

Interviewer: You didn’t use your device much during the hike. Why?

Participant: I didn’t need to check the map often since I’ve done this trail several times. Also, it was warm, so we moved quickly and didn’t stop much for pictures.

Interviewer: When you checked the time, which device did you use?

Participant: Usually my Fitbit for just the time. I might see the time on my phone when I pick it up, but for quick checks, it’s the Fitbit.

Interviewer: You took only two pictures during this hike. Why so few?

Participant: Mostly we were in the trees, so fewer photo opportunities. I took one at a lookout and another of a plant I wanted to identify.

Interviewer: How do you feel being in an environment far from human living spaces?

Participant: I really enjoy it. There’s so much to notice—different plants, animals, sounds, and smells. It’s very alive and different from my daily experience, so I notice more about my surroundings.

Interviewer: Do you prefer hiking on established trails or making your own path?

Participant: I stick to established trails to help preserve the environment and for safety. Sometimes trails are overgrown and require some bushwhacking, but mostly I stay on the trail.

Interviewer: You mentioned carrying an extra battery. Did you use it on this hike?

Participant: No, I used it while driving back. I didn’t need to recharge my phone during the hike.

Interviewer: Why carry an extra battery?

Participant: My phone is old and drains quickly, especially on long hikes. Also, as a precaution in case something happens and I need to navigate or call for help. I can also charge my flashlight with it.

Interviewer: Is an extra battery necessary?

Participant: Not for short hikes, but very useful for longer ones.

Interviewer: How was your experience using the device for recording the hike? Did it distract you?

Participant: It didn’t make a huge difference. I actually looked at my watch less because I had to take it out to record, so I waited longer to check the time. It made me more conscious about device use. It also made me think about keeping a hike journal or notes about what I see.

Interviewer: Did being conscious of the device usage detract from your nature experience?

Participant: Probably somewhat, yes.

Interviewer: You were hiking with others using the same device. How comfortable was that?

Participant: Since my husband was using the same device, it felt normal. We reminded each other when to record. It felt a bit strange when seeing other people on the trail who might think I was talking to myself.

Interviewer: Did checking the time affect how long you wanted to stay hiking?

Participant: Yes. On this hike, we had to get my husband back for an errand, so we checked the time to make sure we could turn around and get back on time.

Interviewer: Does checking the time distract from the wellness you seek in nature?

Participant: Yes, unfortunately.

Interviewer: Do you think it’s possible to hike without checking the time and still meet your goals?

Participant: It depends. Not knowing the time when you have an appointment might be more stressful. But I get a better experience when I don’t have to worry about returning on time. Sometimes I check the time to gauge the heat of the day rather than to plan my return.

Interviewer: You used the map a few times on a trail you know well. Why?

Participant: This trail is less used and overgrown in parts, so I check to make sure I’m on the right path. Also, my phone sometimes loses GPS connection, so I check to ensure it’s recording and doesn’t need restarting.

Participant: Sounds good. I enjoyed participating. Thank you.

Interviewer: We appreciate your time. We’ll reach out when the gift card is ready.

Participant: Thank you. Have a good evening.

Interviewer: You too. Goodbye.

Participant: Goodbye.
