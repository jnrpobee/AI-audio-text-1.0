# Interview Analysis

## Transcript
Interviewer: Tell us about yourself and what hiking means to you.

Participant: I grew up in a very outdoorsy household. My family goes backpacking a couple times every summer, usually as family outings, and we hike a lot. It’s a huge part of who I am. Hiking helps me connect with God and helps me destress, so it’s really important to me.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: I try to hike at least once a week. Whether it’s a three-mile trip or even a one-mile trip, I usually try to go around three to five miles.

Interviewer: What’s your hiking habit like? Do you go alone or with groups?

Participant: I like going with two or three people—smaller groups make it fun.

Interviewer: Do you hike alone?

Participant: Rarely. Sometimes I will, but for safety reasons I usually try not to.

Interviewer: What are the safety reasons?

Participant: Bears, or if I sprained my ankle—things like that could cause a lot of issues.

Interviewer: Have you ever encountered anything like that?

Participant: I’ve seen bears before. Luckily I was with groups. I’ve also seen moose and elk. I haven’t seen a mountain lion, so that’s good.

Interviewer: How is your general technology use?

Participant: I’m on Instagram quite a bit, maybe two hours a day. I also use my phone a lot for school—mostly Safari. Other than that, I don’t really use my phone except for music.

Interviewer: When you go on hikes, how do you prepare?

Participant: I like to look at the trail using a few apps. I use AllTrails quite a bit and sometimes read reviews to make sure it’s a good trail. Then I bring a raincoat, some food, and water.

Interviewer: How do the reviews and trail information affect your planning?

Participant: It helps me feel more comfortable. Usually the only thing I change is the time I go. If a trail is harder, I plan for more time, but I don’t bring anything different.

Interviewer: What technologies do you bring on hikes?

Participant: Usually just my phone. Sometimes I’ll go with AirPods, but if I’m with a group, I tend not to listen to music.

Interviewer: Do you still bring your AirPods with you when you’re with a group?

Participant: No. If I’m going with a group, I don’t bring them.

Interviewer: You said you rarely hike alone. How often do you hike alone?

Participant: Maybe every other month—not a ton.

Interviewer: What apps do you use on your phone while hiking?

Participant: AllTrails and the camera app. I don’t use it for anything else.

Interviewer: At what point do you use AllTrails?

Participant: At the start to make sure I’m going the right way, and when I come across a fork in the trail. Then probably every 15–20 minutes to make sure I’m still on track.

Interviewer: How does it help you during your hike?

Participant: It helps me feel less stressed that I’m not going the wrong way. It makes it more enjoyable knowing it’s basically risk-free.

Interviewer: What motivates you to hike?

Participant: Being out in nature feels peaceful, and I love the fresh air.

Interviewer: What difference does it make for you being in nature versus an urban environment?

Participant: I feel better—less stressed and more focused.

Interviewer: With your use of AllTrails, how does it help you connect with nature?

Participant: I don’t think it directly helps me connect with nature, but it helps me not be stressed so I can connect with nature. It’s a destressor.

Interviewer: About how many hikes have you done so far this year?

Participant: I got back from my mission in June, so it’s only been about four months, but I’ve probably gone on 15 or 20 hikes—around 20.

Interviewer: How long do your hikes last?

Participant: It depends. Yesterday’s hike took about an hour, but a few weeks ago I did a 10-mile hike and that took about 10 hours. It completely depends.

Interviewer: Do you use technology during hikes to determine time, mileage, or anything else besides using AllTrails for navigation?

Participant: I look at it for the time sometimes. Sometimes other people ask how far we’ve gone. On longer hikes, I’ll check the time.

Interviewer: When you say longer hikes, how long do you mean?

Participant: Anything over two hours.

Interviewer: Why is time a factor during those hikes?

Participant: Because I have a lot of things to do outside of hiking, so I want to make sure I’m on schedule with schoolwork and stuff.

Interviewer: On shorter hikes, do you also check the time?

Participant: Not really. Yesterday I didn’t check the time until I got back to the car.

Interviewer: Why not?

Participant: I like the feeling of just being out there and not having to worry about other things—like time or what’s going on in the world.

Interviewer: You mentioned carrying a raincoat. Why do you carry it?

Participant: In case it rains, so I don’t get hypothermia. That’s a big problem.

Interviewer: Do you check the weather before you hike?

Participant: Yes.

Interviewer: How does that affect your plan?

Participant: Sometimes I won’t go if it’s going to rain. I just won’t go in general if it rains because it ruins it and can cause issues. No one likes to hike with wet feet.

Interviewer: If you go on a hike and it suddenly rains, what would you do?

Participant: Put on the raincoat.

## Summary
**Short:** The participant grew up in an outdoorsy family and sees hiking as central to their identity, serving as a way to destress and connect with God. They hike about once a week, typically 3–5 miles, and prefer small groups for enjoyment and safety, citing wildlife and injury risks. Technology use on hikes is minimal and purpose-driven: they rely on AllTrails for navigation and reassurance, plus their camera, and avoid music when hiking with others. Weather strongly shapes planning, as they check forecasts and often skip hikes in rain, carrying a raincoat to prevent getting dangerously cold if conditions change.

**Long:** The participant describes hiking as a core part of who they are, rooted in a childhood of frequent family backpacking trips, and emphasizes its spiritual and mental-health value as a way to connect with God, find peace, and reduce stress. They aim to hike at least weekly (usually 3–5 miles), prefer going with two or three people, and rarely hike alone due to safety concerns such as bears and the risk of injury; they have encountered bears, moose, and elk but not mountain lions. Their general phone use includes Instagram (~2 hours/day) and school-related browsing, but on hikes they keep technology limited to essentials—primarily AllTrails for checking trails and reading reviews beforehand and for in-hike navigation checks (at the start, at forks, and every 15–20 minutes) and the camera—framing AllTrails as a “destressor” that reduces fear of getting lost rather than something that directly enhances nature connection. Time becomes a consideration mainly on hikes longer than two hours because of competing responsibilities like schoolwork, whereas on shorter hikes they avoid checking the time to preserve a sense of escape. Preparation includes reviewing trails and ratings, bringing food and water, checking the weather, and carrying a raincoat; they often avoid hiking in rain due to discomfort and safety concerns, but would use the raincoat if rain begins unexpectedly.

## Follow-up Questions
### Clarifying
- You said hiking helps you “connect with God.” What does that look like on the trail—prayer, quiet reflection, something else?
- When you say you hike “at least once a week,” is that consistent year-round or mostly in certain seasons?
- You mentioned you usually go “around three to five miles,” but sometimes do one mile—what makes you choose a shorter vs. longer route on a given week?
- You said you like going with “two or three people.” Who are those people typically (family, friends, classmates), and how do you decide who to invite?
- You check AllTrails at the start, at forks, and “every 15–20 minutes”—is that on a timer habit, or triggered by uncertainty about the trail?
- You said reviews help you feel comfortable and you mostly only change “the time” you go—what specific review details (trail conditions, wildlife reports, crowds, elevation) influence that timing?
### Probing
- You described AllTrails making the hike feel “basically risk-free.” What risks are you most worried about—getting lost, wildlife like bears, injury like a sprained ankle, or something else?
- You rarely hike alone “for safety reasons” like bears or spraining an ankle—what safety steps do you take even when you’re in a small group (first aid, sharing location, bear spray, telling someone your plan)?
- You said you’ve seen bears (and also moose and elk). How did those encounters affect how you plan hikes now—trail choice, time of day, group size, or what you carry?
- You mentioned you don’t bring anything different for harder trails, just plan more time. What would make you decide to add gear or change preparation (extra water, layers, navigation backup)?
- On hikes over two hours you check time because of schoolwork and being “on schedule.” How do you balance the ‘destress’ goal with the pressure to stay on a timeline?
- You said you don’t use your phone much besides AllTrails and the camera while hiking. What makes taking photos worth it for you, and does it ever pull you out of the ‘peaceful’ feeling you’re seeking?
### Contrast / Compare
- Compare a hike where you’re checking AllTrails every 15–20 minutes versus a hike where you barely look at your phone—how does your stress level and enjoyment differ?
- How does hiking with your usual “two or three people” compare to hiking alone every other month—what changes in safety, pace, conversation, and connection with nature/God?
- When you do bring AirPods (if ever), how does the experience differ with music vs. no music in terms of focus, peace, and awareness of surroundings?
- Contrast shorter hikes (like yesterday’s ~1 hour where you didn’t check time) with longer hikes (like the 10-mile/10-hour one)—what changes in preparation, phone use, and how ‘present’ you feel?
- You said nature makes you feel “less stressed and more focused” compared to an urban environment. What specific elements (sounds, air, views, fewer people) drive that difference for you?
- Compare planning a trail using AllTrails reviews versus going on a familiar trail without checking reviews—how does that change your confidence, timing decisions, and sense of adventure?
