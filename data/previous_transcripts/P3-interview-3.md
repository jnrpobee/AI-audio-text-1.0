# Interview Analysis

## Transcript
Interviewer: Before we get into your hiking experience last week, what does hiking generally mean to you?

Participant: Hiking is a way I enjoy doing different things that are good for me. I like being outdoors, away from the computer and work, to relax and think without constant noise. I enjoy seeing plants, animals, and views. I also like to paint, so I often take pictures on hikes to paint later.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for your hikes?

Participant: I bring enough food, water, and first aid supplies—practical things. I wear layers, use hiking poles, boots, and a backpack. I make sure my phone is fully charged and usually bring an extra battery pack for my phone or flashlight, just in case.

Interviewer: What benefits do you gain from hiking?

Participant: It helps me get physically fit, stronger, and improves my cardio health. I enjoy hiking with others because it’s a good time to talk and bond. Mentally, I always feel refreshed, calmer, and ready to tackle other things after hiking.

Interviewer: When hiking with friends and chatting, does that distract you from feeling refreshed?

Participant: Sometimes I focus more on the conversation and might miss some sights, but often the topics aren’t related to my stress, so it still helps me relax. We usually have periods of chatting and periods of silence to take it all in.

Interviewer: What’s the difference between hiking alone and with friends?

Participant: When alone, I tend to think more and notice my surroundings more. Sometimes I hike without my phone or listen to podcasts. With friends, there’s more social interaction, but also quiet moments.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: Almost every weekend when the weather is nice, not in winter.

Interviewer: How long do your hikes usually last?

Participant: Usually a couple of hours. This year I’ve been busier, so about two to three hours, but in past years I’ve done longer hikes, sometimes six hours or more.

Interviewer: How many hikes do you do in a year?

Participant: Probably 20 to 30, mostly from spring to fall.

Interviewer: Do you hike the same places or different locations?

Participant: About half and half. I revisit some favorite spots yearly but also look for new places.

Interviewer: What motivates you to return to the same places?

Participant: I enjoy those places a lot, and many are close by. I like seeing how the hike changes with the seasons and tracking my own progress as I get stronger.

Interviewer: Do seasonal changes affect your well-being when hiking?

Participant: Yes, I appreciate different things in each season. No one season is better; each has unique qualities.

Interviewer: How do you approach safety when hiking alone?

Participant: I take precautions. I only hike trails I’ve done with a group before, avoid unfamiliar or long trails, and stick to well-traveled paths where I’ll see other people. I use an app called Strava to share my GPS location with family, so they know where I am and when to expect me back.

Interviewer: Besides Strava, do you use other apps or devices to connect with nature or stay focused?

Participant: I use my phone camera to take pictures and a Fitbit watch to track steps and heart rate.

Interviewer: Any other devices you use on hikes?

Participant: No, just my phone and Fitbit.

Interviewer: What’s your main reason for using the camera?

Participant: To capture nice views, interesting patterns, animals, or plants I want to paint later. Also, if I see a plant I don’t know, I take a picture to identify it later.

Interviewer: How do pictures help you connect back to places you’ve hiked?

Participant: I upload them to Google Photos, which sends reminders of when and where I was. Looking back at pictures helps me remember the experience more vividly than just writing it down.

Interviewer: Why don’t you use other devices besides your phone and Fitbit?

Participant: Those cover my needs. I’m not sure what else is out there, but I’d try something new if it could improve my experience.

Interviewer: Without apps like Strava and the camera, do you think you could hike alone safely?

Participant: Sort of. I’d stick to shorter, well-populated places and be out for less time. I wouldn’t feel as confident going farther without location sharing.

Interviewer: What are your preferences for hike difficulty?

Participant: I like challenging hikes. Time is the biggest factor. The longest I’ve done is about 16.5 miles. In summer, a typical hike is six or seven miles. I enjoy shorter hikes too, but if I have a full day, I prefer longer ones. I consider eight miles or more a long hike.

Interviewer: Regarding your recent hike, I want to share your trail map. Is that okay?

Participant: Yes, that’s fine.

Interviewer: We noticed your Strava GPS started after you had already been on the trail for a while. Why didn’t you start it at the trailhead?

Participant: I’m not sure. I thought I did start it at the trailhead. Maybe I activated it late by mistake.

Interviewer: You didn’t use your device much during the hike. Why?

Participant: I didn’t need to check the map often because I’ve done the trail before. Also, it was warm that day, so we moved quickly and didn’t stop much for pictures.

Interviewer: When you checked the time, which device did you use?

Participant: Usually my Fitbit for just the time. I might see the time on my phone when I pick it up, but for quick checks, I use the Fitbit.

Interviewer: You took only two pictures on this hike. Why so few?

Participant: Mostly we were in the trees, so fewer views. I took pictures at a lookout point and of a plant I wanted to identify.

Interviewer: How does being in a natural environment far from human living feel?

Participant: I really enjoy it. There’s so much life—plants, animals, sounds, smells—that’s different from my daily experience. I notice a lot more about my surroundings.

Interviewer: Do you prefer hiking on established trails or making your own path?

Participant: I stick to established trails to help preserve the environment and for safety. Sometimes trails are overgrown, but mostly I stay on them.

Interviewer: You mentioned carrying an extra battery. Did you use it on this hike?

Participant: No, I used it while driving back. My phone didn’t need charging during the hike.

Interviewer: Why carry an extra battery?

Participant: My phone is old and drains quickly, especially on long hikes. It’s also a precaution in case something happens and I need to navigate or call for help. I can also charge my flashlight with it.

Interviewer: Is an extra battery necessary?

Participant: Not for short hikes, but very useful for long ones.

Interviewer: How was your experience using the device to record the hike? Did it distract you?

Participant: It didn’t make a huge difference. I actually checked my watch less often because I had to take out the device to record. It made me more conscious of my device use. It also made me think about keeping a hike journal or notes about what I see.

Interviewer: Did being conscious of device use distract you from your nature experience?

Participant: Probably somewhat, yes.

Interviewer: How comfortable were you using the device while hiking with others?

Participant: Since my husband was using the same device, it didn’t feel weird. We reminded each other when to record. But sometimes it felt strange if other people saw me talking to myself.

Interviewer: Did checking the time affect how long you wanted to stay hiking?

Participant: Yes, especially on this hike because my husband had an errand. We had to watch the time to get back on schedule.

Interviewer: Does checking the time distract from the wellness you seek in nature?

Participant: Yes, unfortunately.

Interviewer: Do you think it’s possible to hike without checking the time and still get back safely?

Participant: It depends. Not knowing the time when you have an appointment might be more stressful. But I get a better experience when I don’t have to worry about time. Sometimes I check the time to gauge the heat of the day rather than to plan my return.

Interviewer: Why did you use the map on a trail you know well?

Participant: The trail is less used and overgrown in parts, so I check to make sure I’m on the right path. Also, my phone sometimes loses GPS connection, so I check the map to keep it recording.

Interviewer: Thank you for participating. We will send you a gift card as compensation once it’s ready.

Participant: Sounds good. I enjoyed participating. Thank you.

Interviewer: Thank you. We’ll reach out when the gift card is ready. Have a good evening.

Participant: You too. Goodbye.

Interviewer: Goodbye.

## Summary
**Short:** The participant views hiking as a restorative outdoor activity that benefits both physical fitness and mental well-being. They prepare carefully with practical gear and use technology like Strava and a Fitbit for safety and tracking, balancing social interaction and solitude on trails. Seasonal changes and familiar versus new trails enrich their experience, while device use sometimes distracts but also encourages mindfulness. Time awareness can detract from relaxation but is necessary for scheduling.

**Long:** The participant describes hiking as a multifaceted activity that promotes physical health, mental clarity, and creative inspiration, especially through photography for later painting. They emphasize thorough preparation with layered clothing, food, and safety devices, including phone apps like Strava for location sharing and a Fitbit for monitoring. Hiking alone allows deeper reflection and nature observation, while group hikes provide social bonding with alternating quiet moments. They hike frequently in spring to fall, revisiting familiar trails to observe seasonal changes and personal progress, while also exploring new locations. Safety is prioritized by choosing well-known, populated trails and sharing GPS data with family. Device use, while helpful for navigation and documentation, sometimes interrupts immersion in nature, and checking time can reduce relaxation, though it remains necessary for managing commitments. Overall, hiking is a cherished, balanced practice that integrates technology, social connection, and solitude to support well-being.
