# Interview Analysis

## Transcript
Interviewer: Before we get into your hiking experience last week, what does hiking generally mean to you?

Participant: I enjoy hiking as a way to do things that are good for me. I like being outdoors, away from the computer and work, to relax and think without constant noise. I enjoy seeing different plants, animals, and views. I also like to paint, so I often take pictures on hikes to paint later.

Interviewer: How do you prepare for your hikes?

Participant: I bring enough food, water, first aid supplies, layers of clothes, hiking poles, boots, and a backpack. I make sure my phone is fully charged and usually bring an extra battery pack for my phone or flashlight, just in case.

Interviewer: What benefits do you gain from hiking?

Participant: It helps me get physically fitter, stronger, and improves my cardio health. I enjoy hiking with others because it’s a good time to talk and bond. Mentally, I always feel refreshed, calmer, and ready to tackle other things after hiking.

Interviewer: When hiking with friends and chatting, does that distract you from feeling refreshed?

Participant: Sometimes I focus more on the conversation and might miss some sights, but usually the topics aren’t stressful, so it still helps me relax. We also have quiet periods during hikes, so it balances out.

Interviewer: What’s the difference between hiking alone and with friends?

Participant: When alone, I tend to think more and notice my surroundings better. Sometimes I hike without my phone or listen to podcasts. With friends, there’s more social interaction.

Interviewer: How often do you hike?

Participant: Almost every weekend when the weather is nice, from spring to fall, about 20 to 30 times a year.

Interviewer: How long do your hikes usually last?

Participant: These days, about two to three hours. In past years, I’ve done longer hikes up to six hours or more.

Interviewer: Do you hike the same places or different locations?

Participant: About half and half. I revisit some favorite spots yearly but also look for new places.

Interviewer: What motivates you to return to the same places?

Participant: I enjoy those places a lot, and they’re usually close by. I like seeing how the hike changes with the seasons and tracking my own progress over time.

Interviewer: Do seasonal changes affect your well-being when hiking?

Participant: Yes, I appreciate different things in each season. No one season is better, but each offers unique experiences.

Interviewer: When hiking alone, how do you handle safety concerns?

Participant: I take precautions by only hiking trails I know well, usually ones I’ve done with a group before. I stick to shorter, well-traveled trails where I’m likely to see other people. I use an app called Strava to share my GPS location with family and tell them when I expect to return.

Interviewer: Besides Strava, do you use other apps or devices to connect with nature or stay focused?

Participant: I use my phone camera to take pictures and a Fitbit watch to track steps and heart rate.

Interviewer: Why do you use the camera on hikes?

Participant: To capture nice views, interesting patterns, or animals so I can remember them and paint later. Also, to identify plants by taking pictures and looking them up afterward.

Interviewer: How do these pictures help you connect back to your hikes?

Participant: I upload them to Google Photos, which sends reminders of where and when I was. Looking back at pictures helps me recall the experience more vividly than just writing notes.

Interviewer: Why don’t you use other hiking devices?

Participant: The phone and Fitbit cover my needs. I’m not sure what else is out there, but I’d try something new if it seemed helpful.

Interviewer: Without apps like Strava and the camera, would you feel safe hiking alone?

Participant: Somewhat. I’d stick to shorter, busier trails and be out for less time. I wouldn’t feel confident going far without location tracking.

Interviewer: What are your preferences for hike difficulty and length?

Participant: I like challenging hikes. Time is the biggest factor. The longest I’ve done is about 16.5 miles. On a day off, I prefer longer hikes—eight miles or more. Otherwise, six or seven miles is typical.

Interviewer: Regarding your recent hike, I want to share your trail map. Is that okay?

Participant: Yes, that’s fine.

Interviewer: We noticed your GPS on Strava started after you had already been hiking for a while. Why?

Participant: I’m not sure. I thought I started it at the trailhead. Maybe I activated it late by mistake.

Interviewer: You didn’t use your device much during the hike. Why?

Participant: It’s a trail I’ve done several times, so I didn’t need to check the map often. Also, it was warm that day, so I moved quickly and didn’t stop much to take pictures.

Interviewer: When you check the time, do you use your Fitbit or phone?

Participant: Just the Fitbit if I’m only checking the time. I might see the time on my phone when I pick it up, but I prefer the watch for time checks.

Interviewer: You took only two pictures on this hike. Why so few?

Participant: Most of the hike was in the trees, so fewer photo opportunities. I took one at a lookout and another of a plant I wanted to identify.

Interviewer: How do you feel being in an environment far from human living spaces?

Participant: I really enjoy it. It’s alive with plants and animals, different sounds and smells. It’s very different from daily life, so I notice more about my surroundings.

Interviewer: Do you prefer hiking on established trails or making your own path?

Participant: I stick to established trails to help preserve the environment and for safety. Sometimes trails are overgrown, but I avoid going off-trail.

Interviewer: You mentioned carrying an extra battery pack. Did you use it on this hike?

Participant: No, I used it on the drive back but didn’t need it during the hike.

Interviewer: Why carry an extra battery?

Participant: My phone battery drains quickly since it’s old, so for longer hikes, I might need it. Also, as a precaution in case of emergencies, to keep my phone or flashlight charged.

Interviewer: Is an extra battery necessary?

Participant: Not for short hikes, but very useful for longer ones.

Interviewer: How was your experience using the device to record your hike? Did it distract you?

Participant: It didn’t make a huge difference. I actually checked my watch less because I didn’t want to interrupt recording. It made me more conscious of device use. It also made me think about keeping a hike journal or notes.

Interviewer: Did being conscious of device use detract from your nature experience?

Participant: Somewhat, yes.

Interviewer: How comfortable were you using the device while hiking with others?

Participant: Since my husband used the same device, it felt normal. We reminded each other when to record. But it felt a bit strange talking to myself when passing other hikers.

Interviewer: Did checking the time affect how long you wanted to stay out hiking?

Participant: Yes, especially on this hike because my husband had an errand, so we needed to leave on time.

Interviewer: Does checking the time distract from the wellness you seek in nature?

Participant: Yes, unfortunately.

Interviewer: Could you hike without checking the time and still enjoy the experience?

Participant: It depends. Not knowing the time with an appointment would be more stressful. But I get a better experience when I don’t have to worry about time. Sometimes I check time to gauge the heat of the day rather than to plan my return.

Interviewer: Why did you use the map on a trail you know well?

Participant: The trail is less used and overgrown in parts, so I check to make sure I’m on the right path. Also, my phone sometimes loses GPS signal, so I check to ensure it’s recording.

Interviewer: Thank you for participating. We will send you a gift card as compensation once it’s ready.

Participant: Sounds good. I enjoyed participating. Thank you.

Interviewer: Thank you. We’ll be in touch. Have a good evening.

Participant: You too. Goodbye.

Interviewer: Goodbye.
